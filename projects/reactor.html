Type: Honors Thesis
Academic: ASU Barrett Honors College

ASU Barrett Honors Thesis project: Redesigned mechanical and electrical systems for a laboratory reactor used to characterize CAM28 energy storage medium. Project Overview

This honors thesis research focused on characterizing CAM28 (a composite of calcium, aluminum, and magnesium oxides), a promising energy storage material with potential applications in high-temperature thermal energy storage systems. The project involved comprehensive system redesign to optimize experimental conditions and enable more detailed characterization of the material's reduction kinetics.

Key Contributions

Mechanical System Redesign
Evaluated and improved the reactor's mechanical architecture to better control reaction conditions, ensure thermal uniformity, and facilitate accurate measurement of reaction kinetics. Design modifications focused on maintaining consistent temperature profiles and minimizing heat losses to the environment.
Electrical System Optimization
Redesigned electrical control and monitoring systems to enable precise temperature control and real-time data acquisition. Improvements provided better feedback for understanding reaction progression and enabled more sophisticated experimental protocols.
Research Findings
Experimental results demonstrated faster reduction reaction rates in CAM28 than previously reported in literature. This finding has significant implications for the practical deployment of CAM28 in thermal energy storage applications, potentially enabling faster charge/discharge cycles and improved system efficiency.
Experimental Methodology
Developed robust experimental procedures for characterizing the material across various operating conditions. Created detailed protocols for temperature ramping, data collection, and analysis that enable reproducible results and comparison with other researchers' findings.

Research Insights

This multi-year research project demonstrates the value of sustained, systematic investigation. By thoughtfully redesigning both mechanical and electrical systems, the project enabled discovery that previous researchers had missed. The faster reduction rates found suggest that operating conditions—not fundamental material limitations—were constraining performance in earlier studies. This emphasizes the importance of rigorous experimental design in materials research.
